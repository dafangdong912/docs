The pressure sensor matrix is the frontline of the PressureLight 压感光护 detection system. Woven invisibly into the garment fabric, these sensors work in the background at all times — distinguishing the normal pressure of movement and activity from the sustained, concentrated pressure that signals you need help. Getting this distinction right is what makes PressureLight reliable rather than noisy.

## What the Pressure Matrix Is

The pressure matrix is a grid of sensors integrated directly into the fabric across the key contact zones of the garment. It continuously samples pressure across the entire grid, building a real-time picture of the forces being applied to the garment. This data streams to the ESP32, which processes it on-device to determine whether what it's seeing is normal activity or a distress event.

## How It Distinguishes Distress from Normal Movement

Not every pressure spike is an emergency — the system is designed to tell the difference:

- **Sustained, concentrated pressure** — a consistent, focused pressure held across one or more sensor zones over time — is the signature the ESP32 looks for as a distress indicator.
- **Rhythmic, distributed movement patterns** — the kind generated by walking, sitting, bending, or exercising — produce varied, shifting pressure profiles that the ESP32 recognises as normal activity and ignores.
- **Threshold algorithm** — the ESP32 applies a pressure-level and duration threshold before declaring an SOS event. A momentary knock or brief contact does not trigger an alert; sustained distress pressure does.

This design means the garment stays silent during normal wear while remaining ready to respond when it truly matters.

## Proper Fit for Accurate Detection

The pressure matrix only works if the sensors are in contact with your body. Fit is critical:

- **Too loose** — sensors lose contact with your body, causing missed triggers. The garment may fail to detect genuine distress pressure.
- **Too tight** — excessive continuous baseline pressure can cause false positives, where the system interprets normal wear as distress.
- **Correct fit** — snug, comfortable, and secure. The garment should move with you without shifting, and you should be able to breathe and move freely.

## Wearing the Garment Correctly

<Steps>
  <Step title="Put on the garment and adjust the fit">
    Dress in the PressureLight and adjust all straps, closures, or fit points until the garment sits evenly against your body with no bunching or gaps at the sensor zones.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Verify the fit feels snug but comfortable">
    The garment should feel secure and close-fitting without restricting your breathing or range of motion. If you feel pressure discomfort at rest, loosen it slightly.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Run a short movement test">
    Walk, sit down, stand up, and bend at the waist. Confirm that normal movement does not trigger any alerts. If you receive a false alarm, adjust the fit before continuing.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Test an intentional SOS trigger">
    Apply deliberate, sustained pressure to the primary sensor zone as described in the SOS triggering guide. Confirm that the LED strip flashes and the vibration motor activates. This verifies that detection is working correctly before you rely on it.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Tip>
  If you experience frequent false alarms during normal wear, the fix is almost always a fit adjustment. Tighten the garment so the sensor zones stay properly aligned against your body, or re-position the garment if the sensor areas have shifted out of place.
</Tip>

<Warning>
  Do not alter, sew over, patch, or apply adhesives to the sensor zones marked on the interior of the garment. Modifying these areas — even with well-intentioned repairs — can degrade sensor sensitivity or cause permanent inaccuracy. If a sensor zone is damaged, contact support.
</Warning>
